Damn this one is quite heavy, Man in his early 30s has drifted through life with about as much emotional baggage as the cast of naruto on steroids. Some of it can be a bit cringe for me especially the language at the beginning and then how he interacts with the love interest. I'm only about 25% percent of the way through so far. I'll let you know how it continues to impact me over time, but as he continues to gain intelligence his life continues to become more dark but also more complex, his naivety through the first parts of the novel leave him to be slowly demystified at all the characters around them, they go from friends, or heroes, or gods, to dark creatures that have not only wronged him but also wrong others all the time. The professors who are self-interested, the thief at the bake shop who also treated him well but created some plot thickening. The girl he likes Mrs. Kinneman it's difficult for him to decide what to do about his feelings and that resonates with me. It's set in sometime back when mental health was treated differently than it is today but not in so far back that it doesn't feel like my own childhood. 
After finishing more of the book, the relationship with the nurse doesn't really continue in the expected way and Charlie finds his own footing in a separate relationship. I believe he has the best intentions to help society but I can't help thinking how dated this book is by the ideas that Daniel uses to portray a genius and including the truly applicable struggles that one faces, and how that starkly contrasts the person Charlie is, so many components of mental health are missing from his ideas. So many worlds apart his happiness is from my own. It makes me wonder. 
Just finished the book yesterday, Charlie was on top of the world mentally for a short time and he developed all these notions about himself. He then quickly lost it all, knowing it's inevitability did not prevent him from still trying to desperately retain whatever intelligence he could. However, there was no choice and he eventually reverted back to his old self. He left an impression on all the people in his life. Was able to see his mom and his sister for how they truly were, and feel sympathy for them even though they were in many ways still wronging him. A scene where he embraces his sister and his mom seeing it and immediately becoming furious and attempting to kill Charlie showed his sister what his mom was truly like. There was a bit stretching. His sister was able to receive some redemption and he got better clarity on the wrongs that others performed on her as well. Overall I'm glad I read it, I think it changed me slightly, if nothing else Charlie's sweet simplicity and willingness to look stupid were his best qualities but as he gained intelligence he lost those attributes, those thoughts pervaded his interactions with the people he cared about and put strain on his relationships. As he continued to lose intelligence he clung to the knowledge as tightly as he could but it wasn't until he stopped trying to cling was he able to regain the parts of him that made him special.
