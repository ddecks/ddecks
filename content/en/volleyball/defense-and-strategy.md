## Defense and Strategy

Watching myself play, I am able to more clearly see where a lot of my weaknesses are. What Lucas said is very true, I believe my block could use a lot of work especially the timing aspect. I do also think I could improve on my jumping ability.

### BLOCKING

The timing aspect is crucial. It's not just about getting up there - it's about when you commit and how you read the attacker. I've noticed watching footage that my block timing is consistently off by just a fraction, which makes all the difference between a stuff block and getting tooled.

I do also think I could improve on my jumping ability. The physical component matters, but the mental read matters even more. When do you commit? What are you taking away? Are you trying to block everything or are you strategic about what you're defending?

### DEFENSE

In all aspects of my game defense could improve. I think to start, placement on the court/positioning would tremendously help my defensive ability. I also want to improve on my ability to dig hard driven attacks.

Right now my defense is lacking more than most of my other skills but honestly it's not that bad either. I'd like to practice my in the pocket defense a lot. I think my reading isn't great so I really need to work on my positioning where and when I have the opportunities.

I'm missing out on lots of hard driven digs that I could be getting, trying to make sure that I'm securing blocks instead of getting tooled.

**Percentages - how many shots go to different spots. What can you take away?**

This is the fundamental question of defense. You can't get everything, so what are you taking away? What are you forcing them to do?

- **Standing still** - taking space and holding it down
- **Guessing** - picking locations to take certain spots/shots
- **Scouting** - studying other team
- **Live reading** - taking what the game circumstances allow
- **Attacker's decision making** - understanding their tendencies
- **Technique** - efficient movement, quicker, athletics
- **Statistics** - knowing the percentages

**Visualization** - as a defender stack your skills to shed attacker advantage

This is key. You're not just reacting. You're stacking advantages:
1. You know their tendencies (scouting)
2. You see the set quality (live reading)
3. You position based on percentages (statistics)
4. You move efficiently (technique)
5. You make a decision (standing still vs guessing)

Each layer removes some of the attacker's advantage until you're in the best possible position to make the play.

### Learning from the Best

Some notes after listening to Taylor and Nick and Chase speak on the subject:

**Taylor's Approach:**

Taylor is not trying to get every play. He knows what he is for sure taking away and then he will try to make the other plays if they become available. There are certain players like Phil where they are going to make it difficult. 

Watch the player not the ball.

This is huge. The ball is the result of the player's decision. If you watch the player, you can anticipate the ball. If you watch the ball, you're always reacting.

**Nick's Strategy:**

Nick is audible when it comes to play call. If they are out of system or the pass isn't great then Phil is going to default towards line and Nick will default angle defense.

Nick is also looking at the window where the attacker will be hitting the ball (strike zone). He also looks at the attacker's approach.

The key here is adaptability. Nick has a base defense, but he adjusts based on what he sees. Out of system? Different defense. Bad pass? Different defense. He's constantly reading and adjusting.

**Chase's Fundamentals:**

Chase iterates that you are passing with a strong base. You want to have your head level and mostly stationary, use it to gather as much information as possible. This also works for serve receive.

Your head is your information gathering tool. If it's bouncing around, you can't process information effectively. Keep it still, keep it level, and let your eyes do the work.

### Teammate Dynamics

How am I setting them up for success? This is the question I should be asking constantly.

- Range of sets
- Covering
- Passing lanes
- Communication verbal/nonverbal
- Giving them vision of the court
- Making executive decisions

Being a good partner isn't just about your individual skills. It's about making your partner better. Can you give them good sets? Can you cover their hits? Can you communicate effectively? Can you make decisions that help the team?

### Mental Game

**Momentum/flow/pressure** - how do you deal with these situations? How do you take advantage of them?

The energy that is brought onto the court is a major reflection of outcome. I've seen this repeatedly. When Scott brought a lot of energy and I resonated well with it, we played smooth volleyball. When the energy was off, we struggled.

**Mentality** - What is your resilience, grit, determination? How is your strategy evolving, adapting? What's your gameplan?

It's true that constantly battling and testing yourself against other people is generally going to result in good things, as long as people are responding to the things they are seeing continuing to adapt and learn and making sure to filter the things they see with the most beneficial possibilities.

**Physicality** - What are you capable of? How have you trained your body? What do you do with your body?

In order to compete with these guys I have to spend multiple days a month sprinting until I'm ready to puke. I just don't know how I'm going to get to that level of commitment and effort right now.

This is the honest truth. The physical component matters. You can have all the strategy in the world, but if you can't execute physically, it doesn't matter.
